{\mktsStyleBold{}a-3}: The L-shape, \cjkgGlue{\cjk{}\cjkgGlue{\cnjzr{}}\cjkgGlue{}}\cjkgGlue{}, is the only binary relationship that occurs in both of

The choice between \cjkgGlue{\cnxJzr{}}\cjkgGlue{} and \cjkgGlue{\cjk{}\cjkgGlue{\cnjzr{}}\cjkgGlue{}}\cjkgGlue{} depends on the element that occupies the left and bottom of the character in

{\mktsStyleBold{}a-15}: The symbol \cjkgGlue{\cjk{}\cjkgGlue{\cnxBabel{}〓}\cjkgGlue{}}\cjkgGlue{} originated in Japanese manual typesetting. When a composer
couldn’t clearly read a character in the manuscript or was unable to find
a certain kanji in the type cases, they’d instead pick any sort and
put it wrong side first onto the composing stick. When galley proofs were
printed, that upside-down sort would leave a mark similar to \cjkgGlue{\cjk{}\cjkgGlue{\cnxBabel{}〓}\cjkgGlue{}}\cjkgGlue{}, making it
visually clear that something was still missing. In the same way, we
use \cjkgGlue{\cjk{}\cjkgGlue{\cnxBabel{}〓}\cjkgGlue{}}\cjkgGlue{} to stand in for an unresolved element in the formulas. For
example, we know the upper left part of \cjkgGlue{\cjk{}\cjkgGlue{\cnxb{}𠪕}\cjkgGlue{}}\cjkgGlue{} is \cjkgGlue{\cjk{}严}\cjkgGlue{}, a fairly common element,
but the lower right part is seemingly not encoded in Unicode, so we write
out the formula \cjkgGlue{\cjk{}\cjkgGlue{\cnxb{}𠪕}\cjkgGlue{}}\cjkgGlue{}:\cjkgGlue{\cnxJzr{}}\cjkgGlue{}\cjkgGlue{\cjk{}严\cjkgGlue{\cnxBabel{}〓}\cjkgGlue{}}\cjkgGlue{}; this is a syntactically valid way of stating what
we can and what we cannot say about that glyph. In other words,
\cjkgGlue{\cjk{}\cjkgGlue{\cnxBabel{}〓}\cjkgGlue{}}\cjkgGlue{} represents the unspecified element as much as an {\mktsStyleItalic{}x\/} in a mathematical
formula represents the unknown quantity.
